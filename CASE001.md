## 2. The finding

### 2.1 Primary finding — Buyer–Supplier Identity (related‑party award)

On tender **`BEL/2026/RADAR/040`** ("Supply of radar sub-assemblies for coastal surveillance"), the **procuring entity** and the **sole awarded supplier** resolve to the **same legal entity**:

| Role | Name as recorded | Resolved CIN |
|---|---|---|
| Procuring entity (buyer) | `Bharat Electronics Limited \|\| Bangalore Complex` | `L32309KA1954GOI000787` |
| Awarded supplier | `Bharat Electronics Limited` | `L32309KA1954GOI000787` |

- **Estimated value:** ₹1,80,00,00,000 (₹180.0 crore)
- **Award value:** ₹1,76,00,00,000 (₹176.0 crore) — 97.8% of estimate
- **Bidders recorded:** 1 (single recorded awardee)
- **Source:** GeM · `https://eprocure.gov.in/eprocure/app?tender=BEL/2026/RADAR/040`

**What this is:** a related‑party / self‑procurement integrity indicator — a buyer awarding a high‑value contract to an entity that resolves to itself. This is a standard audit trigger (relatedparty transaction review, internal‑control test).

**What this is NOT:** proof of wrongdoing. BEL is a Navratna defence PSU; a unit ("Bangalore Complex") awarding to the parent BEL is plausibly a **lawful internal transfer**. SENTRY's context engine recognised this and **down‑graded the indicator from CRITICAL → MEDIUM** with the note: *"Buyer equals a PSU supplier — possible internal procurement; needs review, not presumed adverse."* That restraint is the correct, defensible behaviour.

## 11. Weaknesses (brutally honest)

1. **`single_bidder` is over‑stated.** The dataset has no bid‑count field. "single_bidder" actually means "one recorded *awardee*." Nearly every works/supply contract has one winner, so this indicator fires on almost everything and should be read as weak corroboration, not a red flag. **Recommendation:** rename to `single_recorded_awardee` and gate true single‑bidder on a real bid count.
2. **Engine ceiling / over‑firing.** All five candidates scored critical/92. On sparse (3–6 record) packages, `single_bidder` + `award_clustering` co‑fire too easily. The score ceiling is not discriminating between a related‑party award and routine PSU infrastructure. **This is the single biggest credibility risk for SENTRY** and must be recalibrated before scale.
3. **Pattern vs. context inconsistency.** The context engine down‑graded `buyer_equals_supplier` to *medium* ("likely internal, don't presume adverse"), yet the **Buyer–Supplier Identity Pattern still classifies critical** because pattern rules key on indicator *presence*, not context‑adjusted severity. The headline "critical" therefore partly contradicts the engine's own restraint. **Recommendation:** pattern classification should consume the context‑adjusted severity.
4. **Data provenance.** These are 2026‑dated records with real entity CINs but round values and sequential dates — representative/seed‑grade, not yet live‑verified. The `eprocure.gov.in` URLs follow the genuine GeM/CPPP pattern but were **not fetched live** in this run. CASE #001 is a *methodology demonstration on representative data*; §10 step 1 is mandatory before any external action.
5. **Buyer‑unit granularity.** "BEL ‖ Bangalore Complex" vs. "BEL" may be a normalisation artifact (unit vs. parent collapsed to one CIN). The finding is only as strong as the entity‑resolution boundary, which itself needs the §10 check.
